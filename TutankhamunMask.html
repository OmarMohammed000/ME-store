The Tutankhamun Mask and the Papyrus Painting are two important artifacts from ancient Egypt associated with the pharaoh Tutankhamun

Tutankhamun Mask: The Tutankhamun Mask, also known as the Death Mask of Tutankhamun, is one of the most iconic and recognizable artifacts from ancient Egypt. It was discovered in 1922 in the tomb of Tutankhamun by the archaeologist Howard Carter. The mask is made of solid gold and weighs around 24 pounds (11 kilograms). It depicts the face of the young pharaoh and is adorned with precious gemstones and colored glass inlays. The mask was meant to protect and preserve the pharaoh's face in the afterlife and is considered a masterpiece of ancient Egyptian funerary art.

Papyrus Painting: Papyrus was a material made from the pith of the papyrus plant and was widely used in ancient Egypt for writing and painting. Papyrus paintings were created by applying pigments to the surface of papyrus sheets. These paintings depicted various scenes from Egyptian mythology, daily life, religious rituals, and historical events.

While there are numerous papyrus paintings from ancient Egypt, you might be referring to specific examples associated with Tutankhamun. The tomb of Tutankhamun contained several papyrus scrolls that provided insights into religious rituals, funerary practices, and spells to assist the pharaoh in the afterlife. These papyri included texts such as the Book of the Dead, which contained instructions and prayers for the deceased's journey through the underworld. It's important to note that although Tutankhamun's tomb contained a wealth of treasures, including the famous mask, the papyrus scrolls found were not extensively illustrated or decorated compared to other tombs. Nonetheless, they provided valuable information about ancient Egyptian beliefs and practices.
